# Cash Flow Statement

## What is a Cash Flow Statement?
A Cash Flow Statement tracks the actual movement of cash in and out of a business over a specific period. Unlike the P&L, it focuses purely on cash transactions — not accruals — making it critical for understanding liquidity.

## Three Sections

### 1. Operating Activities
Cash flows from the core business operations.
- Cash received from customers
- Cash paid to suppliers and employees
- Interest and taxes paid
- **Net Cash from Operations** indicates if the business generates enough cash to sustain itself.

### 2. Investing Activities
Cash flows from buying/selling long-term assets and investments.
- Purchase of equipment or property (cash outflow)
- Sale of assets (cash inflow)
- Purchase or sale of investments
- **Negative investing cash flow** often means growth (capital expenditure).

### 3. Financing Activities
Cash flows related to debt, equity, and dividends.
- Proceeds from issuing shares or loans (inflow)
- Repayment of loans (outflow)
- Dividend payments (outflow)
- Share buybacks (outflow)

## Net Change in Cash
**Net Change = Operating + Investing + Financing Cash Flows**
This reconciles to the change in the cash balance on the Balance Sheet.

## Why Cash Flow Matters
- A profitable business can still go bankrupt if it runs out of cash.
- Cash flow is often considered more reliable than profit for assessing financial health.
- Positive operating cash flow is a strong indicator of business sustainability.
- Lenders and investors scrutinize cash flow to assess repayment ability.

## Free Cash Flow (FCF)
**FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures**
Represents cash available after maintaining/expanding assets — a key metric for valuation.
